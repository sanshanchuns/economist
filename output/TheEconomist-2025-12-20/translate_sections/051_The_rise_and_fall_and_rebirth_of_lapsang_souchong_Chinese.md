Visiting the <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">birthplace</span> of black tea can be a challenge. China no longer guards the secrets of its production, as it did in imperial days. But the pine- and bamboo-covered Wuyi hills of northern Fujian are a UNESCO "world heritage site", requiring protection of its cultural relics and flora- and fauna- rich forests. Visitors must be on an <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">authorised</span> trip. Officials get <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">twitchy</span> when a foreigner in a car approaches villages famed for their tea. Anxious minutes pass as they check whether permission has been secured, before waving the vehicle on up the winding road. Deep in the hills is Tongmu, a village and collection of subordinate hamlets scattered across the steep slopes of a valley. It is here that a kind of black tea was born more than 400 years ago—the <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">grand progenitor</span> of all of them, lapsang souchong. What becomes clear in these <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">verdant</span> hills is that lapsang souchong, as it is known in the West, does not exist in China. Even the words lapsang souchong may be a Western <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">concoction</span>. Souchong is based on the local dialect for "small type" (of leaf). The meaning of lapsang is unclear. Here at its birthplace the tea is usually known in (genuine) Chinese as zhengshan xiaozhong. But this too is <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">misleading</span>. Zhengshan xiaozhong can have a trace of the smokiness of lapsang souchong, but it is <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">far superior</span> in taste.

The Victorians <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">knew no better</span>. Long before chop suey and chow mein entered common usage, evoking cheap bland fare, British tea-drinkers were <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">venerating</span> lapsang souchong. In London's 19th-century tea-rooms, it hinted at something <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">posh</span>. Victorian advertising called the tea an <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">enhancer</span> of cognitive ability for "men of brain power", much in the way faddish supplements are hawked on podcasts today. In 1885, one ad quoted Queen Victoria as urging women to try it, too.

As it became <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">fused</span> with Western culture, tea—a Chinese invention—lost much of its original character. Milk and sugar were added, <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">corrupting</span> the <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">subtleties</span> and varieties of flavour that Chinese consumers relish. But lapsang souchong remained a favourite of more <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">refined</span> types, often without milk. Britain's wartime leader, Winston Churchill, is said to have been a fan (though evidence is lacking). So, too, it is told, was JP Morgan, the American banker.

In London the Evening Standard called it "one of those terrible misjudgments that business schools may well be brooding over in decades to come".

The problem is not with the tea farmers in the Wuyi hills. They are still <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">harvesting</span> the leaves from small bushes on rugged slopes, as generations before them have been doing. It is not a job for machines: humans are needed to identify the right leaves to be picked (four or five down from the tip of the branch). Your correspondent's <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">clumsy</span> attempts were no match for those of a <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">dexterous</span> group of caichanu, as female tea-pickers are known, who <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">gamely</span> let him try. They are in their 60s—younger folk prefer work in the cities.

The <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">painstaking</span> efforts of the caichanu are matched by those of the men who treat the leaves. Jiang Junfan, who is in his mid-60s, belongs to a family that has been doing this for 24 generations (he says he is training the 25th). He shows off the <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">qinglou</span> where he works: a traditional three-storey wooden building where the leaves are <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">pressed</span> and smoked. There are only two like it in Tongmu. Tea traders say that these days, the lapsang souchong that most foreigners drink is sourced from elsewhere in China, and perhaps from other countries. Instead of wood, some use chemical substitutes to produce the smoky flavour. Wuyi's <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">meticulously</span> produced tea is far more expensive.

And Western <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">palates</span> are changing. In China, meanwhile, zhengshan xiaozhong thrives. China's decades-long economic boom has created a sizeable market of wealthy customers who are willing to pay <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">exorbitant</span> prices for the genuine, pinewood-smoked article. And villagers in Tongmu have responded to market forces with <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">zeal</span>. In 2005 they invented a new form of black tea called Jin Jun Mei (Golden Eyebrow), which uses the tender unopened buds of leaves from the same bushes that produce standard zhengshan xiaozhong a little later in spring. They are not smoked. A pot of this <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">exquisite</span>-tasting tea can sell for about one-quarter of the price of gold.

There is a <span style="color:#A24C4A; font-weight:bold; text-decoration:underline;">snag</span> for zhengshan xiaozhong producers in Tongmu. To protect Wuyi's forests, the authorities have imposed tight controls on the use of pinewood.
